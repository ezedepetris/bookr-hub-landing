Staffing Models

There are three main ways to structure barbershop staffing, each with different cost structures and profit potential.

Booth Rental

The barber pays you a fixed rent to use the chair or booth, while they often get to keep their service money. This provides predictable income regardless of daily traffic.

Commission

The barber earns a percentage of each service (typically 40-60%). This is a variable cost that scales with business volume.

Employee

You pay the barber a specific hourly rate or salary. This offers the most control but requires careful scheduling to maintain profitability.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to start a barbershop?
Startup costs range from $50,000 for a compact 1-2 chair setup to $200,000+ for a premium multi-chair shop. Your biggest cost drivers are the number of chairs, lease and renovation expenses, and staffing model.
What license do I need to open a barbershop?
Requirements vary by location. Most areas require a barber license (1,000-2,000 hours training), a business license, liability insurance, and health department permits. Check your local regulations before opening.
Should I rent chairs to barbers or hire employees?
Booth rental provides predictable income with less risk. Hiring employees gives you more control but requires careful scheduling. Many owners start with booth rental and transition as they learn the business.
How long does it take for a barbershop to become profitable?
Most barbershops break even within 12-18 months. Key factors include chair utilization rate (target 80%+), pricing strategy, and client retention. Online booking with pre-payment improves cash flow significantly.
